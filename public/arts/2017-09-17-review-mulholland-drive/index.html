Review: Mulholland Drive

Thais Baracat. September 17 2017.

“A film is going to hit somebody or it’s not. There’s nothing you can do about it”. From authorship by David Lynch, the previous statement summarizes the director’s perseverance to deliver his unique pieces of art. And this pattern would not be different when it comes to Mulholland Drive.

Released in 2001, the ingeniously film arise from a woman involved in a car accident in the sinuous geometry of Mulholland Drive. After rendering her amnesic, she is joined by an enthusiastic Hollywood hopeful so as to search for clues across L.A., in a journey riddled with dreams and reality.

Wrapped in this surrealist atmosphere, the dichotomy between objectivity and subjectivity is set through a painstakingly precise shooting. In order to evoke illusory facts, the image stability is abandoned, and a hand camera introduces a skewed scene. In addition, this is combined with unusual angles and alternating focus, if not calculated cuts. Photography has also its subtlety, in which lightening is used as a feature of subversion, thus leaving his work hauntingly moving even when nothing happens. Naomi Watts’ great acting should be recalled as well.

Indeed, the only feature that reminds us we’re still watching the same film is the characters, being nearly impossible to conceive of such utterly impressive piece without of meticulous care. As acknowledged by “David Lynch: The Man from Another Place”, Lynch despises every attempt of interpretation of his work. Nonetheless, one should not be misled to assume it has an “open plot”: much on the contrary, once everything showed – from objects to clothes – has a meaningful purpose.

For those who suffer from bad memory or are used to timely, logic and linear narratives, the 147 minutes can be rather daunting, when not tiresome. The indispensable recollection of multiple elements and the dense detailed plot raise questions whether the film is accessible. All in all, it’s undeniable its ability to anchor in our minds.
